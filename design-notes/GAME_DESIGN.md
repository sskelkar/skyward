# FLIGHT PATH: A D&D-Inspired Agentic Management Campaign

## Campaign Title: "Flight Path: The Director's Gambit"

---

## 1. NARRATIVE FRAMEWORK: The Hero's Journey (Startup Edition)

### Campaign Arc Structure (4-8 Turns)

**Act I: The Call to Adventure (Turns 1-2)**
- **Turn 1: "Founding Day"** - The airline dream is born. Venture capital secured. Board expectations set. Directors introduced.
- **Turn 2: "First Blood"** - Initial systems go live. First customers. First failures. Reality hits.

**Act II: Trials and Tribulations (Turns 3-5)**
- **Turn 3: "The Pressure Mounts"** - Market forces emerge. Competitors attack. Internal conflicts arise.
- **Turn 4: "Dark Night of the Soul"** - Major crisis. System failures cascade. Team cohesion tested.
- **Turn 5: "The Pivot"** - Strategic decisions made. Departments forced to integrate or die.

**Act III: The Final Push (Turns 6-8)**
- **Turn 6: "Launch Prep"** - Systems hardening. Integration testing. Board breathing down necks.
- **Turn 7: "Launch Day"** - The airline goes live. Chaos ensues. Directors manage fires.
- **Turn 8: "Epilogue & Reckoning"** - Post-launch metrics. Victory or failure. Lessons learned.

### Narrative Spine

Every great D&D campaign needs a central conflict. Here it's not just "launch an airline" - it's:

**"Can a team of brilliant engineers transform from builders to orchestrators before the money runs out?"**

The meta-narrative is about mindset shift. Each director starts thinking "I'll code this myself" and must evolve to "I'll direct my agents to build this."

## 2. CHARACTER CREATION: Director Archetypes

### Character Sheet Components

Each Director defines their character using this template:

#### A. Background Story (Narrative Hook)
- **Previous Role**: What were you before joining this startup?
  - Ex: "Senior engineer at Google, burnt out on bureaucracy"
  - Ex: "Failed founder, hungry for redemption"
  - Ex: "Fresh grad, naive optimism intact"

#### B. Department & Domain
Choose one:
1. **Director of Ticketing** - Customer-facing booking system
2. **Director of Inventory** - Aircraft, routes, seat management
3. **Director of Recommendations** - Personalization, upsells, ML systems
4. **Director of Advertising** - Customer acquisition, marketing tech
5. **Director of Operations** - Infrastructure, monitoring, reliability
6. **Director of Data** - Analytics, reporting, business intelligence

#### C. Archetype (D&D Class Equivalent)
This determines playstyle and special abilities:

- **The Architect** (Wizard): Master planner, documentation-driven, thinks 3 turns ahead
  - *Special Ability*: Once per game, can "rewind" an agent task that failed
  - *Weakness*: Analysis paralysis, slow to ship

- **The Scrapper** (Fighter): Moves fast, breaks things, fixes them faster
  - *Special Ability*: Can deploy twice in one turn, but with technical debt
  - *Weakness*: Creates integration headaches for others

- **The Diplomat** (Bard): Master collaborator, builds bridges between departments
  - *Special Ability*: Can "borrow" another director's agent for one task
  - *Weakness*: Own department sometimes neglected

- **The Perfectionist** (Paladin): Quality above all, slow but reliable
  - *Special Ability*: Systems have 50% fewer bugs, but take 25% longer
  - *Weakness*: Misses deadlines when quality isn't met

- **The Chaos Agent** (Rogue): Experimental, tries wild ideas, high risk/reward
  - *Special Ability*: Can attempt "impossible" tasks with 40% success rate
  - *Weakness*: Spectacular failures that affect other departments

#### D. Starting Resources (Character Stats)

Each director starts with:
- **Agent Budget**: 5 agent-tasks per turn (grows over time)
- **Technical Debt**: 0 (accumulates based on choices)
- **Trust Capital**: 10 points (used to influence other directors)
- **Innovation Tokens**: 2 (spend to try risky new approaches)

#### E. Personal Quest (Side Story)

Each director has a secret personal goal:
- "Prove myself to the co-founder who doubted me"
- "Build a system so elegant it gets open-sourced"
- "Don't let my team see me fail like at my last startup"
- "Ship something before my visa expires"

These create roleplaying opportunities and tension.

## 3. SCENARIO DESIGN: 7 Compelling Events

These are your "dungeon encounters" - each forces decisions and reveals character.

### Event 1: "The Investor Audit" (Turn 2)
**Setup**: Lead investor announces surprise technical due diligence. All systems reviewed next turn.

**Pressure**:
- Directors can focus on making things "look good" vs. "work well"
- Temptation to hide technical debt
- Must decide: impress investor or build right foundation?

**Decision Points**:
- Spend agents polishing demos vs. fixing core issues
- Collaborate to present unified vision vs. each shine individually
- Tell truth about delays vs. overpromise

**D&D Parallel**: This is like the "corrupt noble inspecting the party" - do you deceive, comply, or rebel?

---

### Event 2: "The Competitor Leak" (Turn 3)
**Setup**: TechCrunch publishes that MegaAir is launching same concept in 6 months with 100M funding.

**Pressure**:
- Timeline compressed - must launch first or be irrelevant
- Feature creep temptation - "we need to match their AI chatbot!"
- Morale hit - "why bother if they'll crush us?"

**Decision Points**:
- Cut scope to ship faster vs. maintain feature parity
- Focus on differentiation vs. direct competition
- Directors can burn Innovation Tokens to build "secret weapons"

**D&D Parallel**: The "rival adventuring party" - do you race them, undermine them, or find different treasure?

---

### Event 3: "The Cascading Failure" (Turn 4)
**Setup**: Inventory system has a bug that corrupts data. Ticketing depends on Inventory. Recommendations depends on Ticketing. Everything breaks.

**Pressure**:
- This is inter-department failure - who's responsible?
- Must collaborate to fix, but blame game starts
- Can't move forward until resolved

**Decision Points**:
- Inventory director: admit fault and fix vs. deflect blame
- Dependent directors: help fix root cause vs. build workarounds
- All: spend 2 turns fixing properly vs. 1 turn quick patch

**Mechanical Effect**:
- Proper fix: Departments get "Integration Bonus" (+2 agent tasks/turn when working together)
- Quick patch: Accumulate 5 Technical Debt, problems resurface in Turn 6

**D&D Parallel**: The "cursed artifact" that's affecting the whole party - remove curse together or suffer individually?

---

### Event 4: "The Regulation Hammer" (Turn 5)
**Setup**: FAA announces new data privacy rules. Customer data handling must be completely revamped. Affects all departments.

**Pressure**:
- Unexpected work that wasn't planned
- Compliance is non-negotiable (legal stakes)
- Different departments interpret rules differently

**Decision Points**:
- Data Director takes lead and others follow vs. each interpret independently
- Build proper compliance framework vs. minimum viable compliance
- Delay launch to get it right vs. launch and patch

**Special Twist**: Game runner introduces "Compliance Auditor" NPC who becomes recurring character - friendly if you engage early, adversarial if you ignore.

**D&D Parallel**: "The king's decree" - quest objective changes mid-campaign, party must adapt.

---

### Event 5: "The Talent Raid" (Turn 6)
**Setup**: MegaAir tries to poach your best engineers. One director loses 2 agent-tasks for next turn unless they counter-offer.

**Pressure**:
- Resource scarcity - someone will suffer
- Must choose: match offer (spend Innovation Tokens) vs. let them go
- Other directors can "loan" resources, but creates debt

**Decision Points**:
- Who loses the agents? (Directors vote or negotiate)
- How to redistribute work?
- Build morale-boosting initiative together vs. focus on retention individually

**Mechanical Effect**:
- If directors collaborate to build shared "culture system", all gain +1 agent task permanently
- If they fight, everyone loses 1 Innovation Token (bitterness)

**D&D Parallel**: "The party is separated" - resource management when you're weakened.

---

### Event 6: "The Launch Day Disaster" (Turn 7)
**Setup**: Launch begins. Immediately, unexpected load patterns. Systems behave differently in production. Three critical bugs emerge simultaneously across departments.

**Pressure**:
- Real-time crisis - decisions must be made THIS session
- Customer impact - every minute down is reputation loss
- Must triage: what gets fixed first?

**Decision Points**:
- Directors can "heroically code" themselves (breaks the game's spirit, but works)
- Or orchestrate agents under pressure (slower but teaches the lesson)
- Sacrifice one feature to save another

**Mechanical Innovation**: This turn happens in "real-time rounds"
- Each director gets 3 action rounds
- Must decide each round: direct agents, coordinate, or execute
- Other directors can donate their rounds to help

**Victory Condition**: If 4+ directors resist urge to code themselves and successfully orchestrate agents through crisis, they "unlock the true ending."

**D&D Parallel**: The "final boss fight" - coordinated action, resource management, heroic choices.

---

### Event 7: "The Acquisition Offer" (Turn 8 - Epilogue)
**Setup**: MegaAir, impressed by your scrappy launch, offers to acquire you. Board says decide.

**Pressure**:
- Different directors have different incentives (personal quests matter here)
- Is this victory or selling out?
- What does success even mean?

**Decision Points**:
- Accept (financial win, but your baby dies)
- Reject (maintain independence, harder road ahead)
- Counter-offer (partnership instead of acquisition)

**Narrative Payoff**: This isn't a "correct" answer - it's reflection on what was learned. The real victory is whether they shifted to agentic thinking.

**D&D Parallel**: "The king offers you lordship" - do you accept the comfort or keep adventuring?

## 4. COLLABORATION MECHANICS: Party Dynamics

In D&D, solo players die. Same here. Design mechanics that FORCE collaboration:

### A. Integration Dependencies (Mechanical)
Create a dependency graph at game start:

```
Ticketing → Inventory (can't sell seats that don't exist)
Recommendations → Ticketing (need booking data to recommend)
Advertising → Recommendations (need to know what to promote)
Operations → All (everything needs infrastructure)
Data → All (everyone needs metrics)
```

**Rule**: You cannot complete your turn goals unless your dependencies are met.

**Example**:
- Turn 3 Goal: "Ticketing must launch booking flow"
- But Inventory hasn't provided seat availability API yet
- Ticketing Director must either:
  - Help Inventory finish (spend 1 agent-task on their work)
  - Build temporary mock data (accumulate Technical Debt)
  - Negotiate timeline change (spend Trust Capital)

### B. Shared Resource Pool

Each turn, there's a "Company Pool" of 3 bonus agent-tasks.

**Rule**: Can only be claimed if 2+ directors agree on how to use them.

**Effect**: Forces negotiation, coalition-building, politics (the fun kind).

**Scenarios**:
- Two directors both need them - must compromise
- One director volunteers them for another - builds alliance
- All agree to spend on shared infrastructure - teamwork!

### C. The "Stand-Up" Ritual

Each turn begins with a mandatory sync session:
1. Each director states: "What I shipped" / "What I'm shipping" / "What blocks me"
2. Other directors can offer help, ask for help, or flag conflicts
3. Game runner introduces the turn's event here

**Mechanical Effect**: Directors who actively help others get +1 Trust Capital.

**Roleplaying Effect**: Creates the "party banter" feel of D&D.

### D. Alliance Tokens

New mechanic: Directors can form temporary alliances.

**How it works**:
- Two directors agree to an alliance for one turn
- Allied directors can share agent-tasks freely
- Both get +1 to success rolls (systems work better when designed together)
- But: both share blame if either fails

**Strategic Depth**:
- Do you ally with struggling director (risky but builds trust)?
- Do you ally with strong director (safe but less impactful)?

### E. The "Blame Game" Negative Mechanic

When systems fail, someone must be blamed (by the board/investors).

**Rule**:
- Directors vote who's responsible (can't vote for yourself)
- Blamed director loses 2 Trust Capital
- BUT: Other directors can volunteer to "share blame" (each loses 1 instead)

**Purpose**: Tests character. Will you throw colleague under bus or show solidarity?

**D&D Parallel**: "The town blames the party for the fire" - unified front or scapegoat?

## 5. TENSION & STAKES: Creating Drama

Business sims are boring. D&D is exciting. Here's how to bring tension:

### A. The Doom Clock (Visible Stakes)

Create a visual "Launch Readiness" tracker:
- Starts at 0%
- Must reach 100% by Turn 7
- Each completed goal adds 10-15%
- Each failure subtracts 5-10%

**Dramatic Effect**: Everyone can SEE if they're on track. Tension builds naturally.

**Twist**: At 80%, board gets nervous and introduces new requirement (moves goalpost).

### B. Personal Failure States (Individual Stakes)

Each director has a "Credibility" score (separate from Trust Capital).

**Drops when**:
- Your system causes cascading failure
- You miss deadlines 2 turns in a row
- Other directors vote "no confidence"

**Consequences**:
- At 3: Board questions your leadership
- At 1: You're "reassigned" (game over for that player)
- At 0: You're fired (dramatic exit, player can become "consultant" NPC)

**Why this works**: Real stakes make decisions matter. Fear of elimination creates tension.

### C. The Betrayal Option (Social Stakes)

At Turn 5, game runner privately offers each director a "betrayal" opportunity:

**Examples**:
- "MegaAir will pay you $500k to leak our launch strategy"
- "Another director has been badmouthing you to the board - retaliate?"
- "You can take credit for another director's work at the investor meeting"

**Rule**: Betrayals are secret until revealed. Creates paranoia and drama.

**Payoff**: If NO ONE betrays, all directors get bonus ending. If anyone betrays, creates dramatic confrontation.

**D&D Parallel**: The "evil character in good party" tension, but temporary and game-bounded.

### D. The Moral Dilemma Events (Ethical Stakes)

Not every decision should be technical. Introduce ethical quandaries:

**Event: "The Data Mining Opportunity"**
- Advertising Director discovers they can boost conversions 30% by using borderline-unethical data practices
- Legal says "technically allowed" but Data Director says "morally wrong"
- Board says "do what it takes to hit revenue targets"

**Decision**: Revenue vs. ethics. Directors must debate and vote.

**Consequence**: Choice affects public perception in later turns (karma system).

**Event: "The Accessibility Trade-off"**
- Building proper accessibility will take 1 full turn of effort
- Only affects 2% of customers
- Investor says "nice to have, not need to have"

**Decision**: Inclusion vs. speed.

**Consequence**: If skipped, game runner introduces "journalist investigating accessibility" in Turn 7 (scandal risk).

**Why this works**: D&D is memorable when players face ethical choices, not just tactical ones.

### E. The MVP Temptation (Scope Stakes)

Every turn, directors are tempted to cut corners:

**"Quick Win" Cards** (offered by game runner):
- "Ship with mock data instead of real integration" (save 1 turn, gain 3 Technical Debt)
- "Copy competitor's UX instead of designing your own" (save 2 agent-tasks, lose differentiation)
- "Skip testing and fix bugs in production" (ship faster, but 50% chance of Turn 7 disaster)

**Purpose**: Teaches that "move fast and break things" has real consequences.

**Tracking**: Technical Debt accumulates and "explodes" at Turn 7 if >15 total.

## 6. SESSION STRUCTURE: The Turn Ritual

Each turn should feel like a D&D session - structured but dynamic.

### Pre-Session (5 min)
**Game runner sends async**:
- Recap of last turn's outcomes
- This turn's event/scenario
- Updated company metrics (Doom Clock, resources, etc.)

### Session Start (10 min)
**"The Stand-Up"**
1. Each director reports (in character):
   - "What my agents delivered last turn"
   - "What I'm tackling this turn"
   - "What I need from others"

2. Game runner introduces the turn's dramatic event
   - Narrate it dramatically (not just "here's a problem")
   - Example: "The Slack channel explodes at 3am. Inventory system is down. Ticketing Director Sarah sees the booking flow returning 500 errors. By sunrise, the CEO is asking questions. What do you do?"

### Planning Phase (15 min)
**"The War Council"**

Directors discuss strategy:
- Debate how to respond to event
- Negotiate resource sharing
- Form alliances or call in favors
- Make moral/strategic decisions

**Game runner role**:
- Ask provocative questions ("But what if Inventory can't fix it in time?")
- Introduce NPC perspectives (board, customers, investors)
- Let them argue - conflict is content

### Execution Phase (20 min)
**"The Agent Orchestration"**

Each director describes:
1. **The Goal**: What outcome they're trying to achieve
2. **The Agent Plan**: How they'll direct agents to achieve it
   - "I'll have Agent-1 design the API schema"
   - "Agent-2 will implement it"
   - "Agent-3 will write integration tests"
3. **The Integration**: How it connects to other departments

**Game runner role**:
- Challenge unrealistic plans ("That's 8 agent-tasks but you have 5")
- Ask for details ("How will your agent know what schema Inventory needs?")
- Make them think like managers, not engineers

**Mechanical Resolution**:
- Simple success check (roll or game runner decides based on plan quality)
- Partial success = works but with complications (Technical Debt)
- Failure = must retry next turn or seek help

### Outcome Phase (10 min)
**"The Aftermath"**

Game runner narrates results:
- Successes are celebrated (describe the working system)
- Failures are dramatized (describe the bug, the customer complaint, the CEO's frown)
- Update trackers (Doom Clock, Trust Capital, Technical Debt, etc.)

**Introduce next turn's hook**:
- End on cliffhanger ("As you're celebrating the successful integration, an email arrives from the FAA...")
- Set up tension ("You've completed 60% of launch prep, but MegaAir just announced they're launching in 2 months, not 6...")

### Post-Session (Async)
**"The Reflection"**

Each director submits:
- Journal entry (in character) about the turn
- One thing they learned about agentic management
- One decision they'd change

**Game runner reviews and preps next turn's drama**.

## VICTORY CONDITIONS & ENDINGS

### Multiple Endings (D&D Style)

**The Glorious Launch** (Best Ending)
- Achieved if: Doom Clock >90%, Technical Debt <10, all directors still employed, no betrayals
- Narrative: "Your airline launches smoothly. Press is positive. Customers are delighted. Six months later, you're profitable and hiring. You've built something beautiful, and you've learned to lead agents, not code for them."

**The Scrappy Survival** (Good Ending)
- Achieved if: Doom Clock >70%, Technical Debt <20, 4+ directors still employed
- Narrative: "Launch day is rocky. Systems crash twice. But your team rallies. You fix things in real-time. It's not pretty, but it works. You're alive, you're learning, and you're not giving up."

**The Pyrrhic Victory** (Bittersweet Ending)
- Achieved if: Doom Clock >70%, but Technical Debt >20 or multiple directors fired
- Narrative: "You launched, but at what cost? The systems work, barely. Half your leadership team is gone. You're haunted by the shortcuts you took. The airline runs, but you wonder if it was worth it."

**The Graceful Pivot** (Alternative Ending)
- Achieved if: Directors vote to delay launch (requires unanimous decision at Turn 5+)
- Narrative: "You told the board you needed more time. They were furious. But you stood firm. Three months later, you launch something truly remarkable. Slower, but better. You've learned that sometimes the right answer is 'not yet'."

**The Acquisition** (Neutral Ending)
- Achieved if: Directors accept MegaAir's offer
- Narrative: "You sold. The payday is life-changing. But as you walk out of the office for the last time, you wonder what might have been. You've learned to manage agents. Now what will you build next?"

**The Catastrophic Failure** (Bad Ending)
- Achieved if: Doom Clock <50% by Turn 7, or Technical Debt >30
- Narrative: "Launch day is a disaster. Systems fail catastrophically. Customers demand refunds. The board shuts it down. But you learned something valuable: great technology isn't enough. Leadership, collaboration, and good judgment matter more."

## TEACHING MOMENTS (Meta-Game)

After each session, game runner can briefly break the fourth wall:

**"What did we just learn about agentic management?"**

Examples:
- Turn 2: "Notice how planning the agent tasks took longer than you expected? That's real. Management is about clear direction, not just delegation."
- Turn 4: "When the integration broke, you had to debug someone else's agent's work. That's collaboration in agentic systems - you need shared understanding."
- Turn 7: "Under pressure, you wanted to code it yourself. That's natural. But you resisted and orchestrated instead. That's the mindset shift."

## CUSTOMIZATION & REPLAY VALUE

### Difficulty Levels

**Story Mode**:
- Generous resources, forgiving failure states
- Focus on narrative and learning
- Recommended for first-time players

**Survival Mode**:
- Stricter resources, harsh consequences
- Events are more frequent and severe
- For experienced players who want challenge

**Chaos Mode**:
- Random events every turn
- PvP elements (directors can actively sabotage each other)
- For players who want maximum drama

### Modular Event Deck

Game runner maintains a deck of 20+ events and draws them based on:
- Company state (struggling = more crisis events)
- Director choices (unethical choice = journalist event later)
- Turn number (early = growth challenges, late = crisis management)

This makes each playthrough different.

### Legacy Elements

Between campaigns, track:
- Which directors "succeeded" (affects starting resources in next campaign)
- What lessons were learned (unlocks new archetypes)
- Technical debt that "carries over" (if playing with same team, their past choices haunt them)

## FINAL THOUGHTS: Why This Works

**As a D&D gamerunner, here's why this campaign structure succeeds:**

1. **Character-driven**: Directors aren't just roles, they're characters with motivations, flaws, and arcs
2. **Moral stakes**: Not just "launch the airline" but "who do we want to be?"
3. **Collaborative tension**: Like a D&D party, you need each other but don't always agree
4. **Meaningful choices**: No "correct" answers, only trade-offs
5. **Narrative payoff**: The story you create together is the real reward
6. **Teachable moments**: The lessons emerge from play, not lectures

**The secret sauce**: You're not teaching "how to use Claude agents." You're running a campaign where the only way to win is by learning to use Claude agents. The learning is inevitable because it's embedded in the gameplay.

Welcome to Flight Path. The board is counting on you. Your agents await orders. The competition is closing in.

What do you do?

## APPENDIX: Sample Opening Narration

*Game runner reads this at Turn 1, Session 1:*

"Six months ago, you were strangers. Now you're Directors - the leadership team of Phoenix Airlines, the startup that's going to revolutionize air travel. Or so you told the investors.

The conference room smells like fresh paint and ambition. Your co-founder stands at the whiteboard, marker in hand. 'We have one year,' she says. 'One year to build an airline from nothing. Ticketing, inventory, recommendations, advertising, operations, data - all of it. The board thinks we need 200 engineers. We have 12. And you.'

She points to each of you in turn. 'But you're not here to code. We don't have time for that. You're here to lead. Each of you commands a team of Claude agents - the most powerful development force ever created. Your job isn't to build the systems yourself. It's to orchestrate your agents to build them. Faster and better than anyone thought possible.'

She pauses. Looks each of you in the eye. 'MegaAir has a billion-dollar war chest. Legacy carriers want us dead. Regulators are watching. Our runway is 12 months. After that, we're either flying or falling.'

The marker squeaks on the whiteboard. She writes: Q1 GOALS.

'So, Directors. What are you going to ship this quarter? And how will your agents make it happen?'

The game has begun."
